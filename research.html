Research

My research uses strangeness — hyperons and kaons — as a probe of the strong interaction and of dense nuclear matter, combining precision spectroscopy with original detector techniques.

Hypernuclear Physics

Replacing a nucleon with a hyperon gives access to the hyperon–nucleon (YN) interaction and to baryon–baryon forces that are otherwise difficult to probe — with direct implications for the equation of state of neutron stars.

  • J-PARC E73 — the hypertriton lifetime puzzle. As experiment spokesperson, I lead a measurement based on the spin–non-flip reaction $^3\mathrm{He}(\mathrm{K}^-,\pi^0)\,^3_\Lambda\mathrm{H}$, in which a PbF₂ calorimeter tags the forward-boosted π⁰ through a single high-energy γ. The \(^{4}_{\Lambda}\mathrm{H}\) lifetime ($\tau = 206 \pm 8 \pm 12$ ps) and the \(^{3,4}_{\Lambda}\mathrm{H}\) production cross sections are published; the \(^{3}_{\Lambda}\mathrm{H}\) lifetime analysis is ongoing.
  • Short-range correlations with the Λ hyperon (SPring-8 LEPS2). As proposal spokesperson, I pioneer the use of the isospin-zero Λ as a probe of nucleon short-range correlations and the tensor-force origin of the EMC effect, with the recoiling proton measured in a large-acceptance TPC.
  • Spin-dependent ΛN interaction. During my Ph.D. (2004–2009) I discovered five new γ-ray transitions from ${}_{\Lambda}^{12}\mathrm{C}$ and ${}_{\Lambda}^{11}\mathrm{B}$ hypernuclei, determining the spin-dependent terms of the ΛN interaction.

Kaon-related Hadron Physics

Antikaons carry an s quark and can bind to nucleons, forming kaonic atoms and exotic states that reveal the antikaon–nucleon interaction below threshold.

  • First evidence for the deeply bound $\mathrm{K}^{-}\mathrm{pp}$ kaonic nuclear state (J-PARC E15).
  • Determination of the $\Lambda(1405)$ pole position via $d(\mathrm{K}^{-}, n)\pi\Sigma$ (J-PARC E31).
  • First observation of $\Lambda\pi^{\pm}$ signals near the $\bar{K}N(I=1)$ threshold at Belle (first author).
  • Sub-eV strong-interaction measurements in kaonic-helium atoms with TES microcalorimeters (J-PARC E62).

Antideuteron–Nucleus Interactions

The interplay of antimatter and matter is tied to one of the deepest open questions in physics — the matter–antimatter asymmetry. While antiprotons are well studied, antinuclei such as the antideuteron (d̄) remain largely unexplored. As proposal spokesperson, I lead the first dedicated study of the d̄ at the J-PARC K1.8 beam line.

  • Antideuteron–nucleus potential: how an additional antinucleon changes the d̄–nucleus potential relative to the p̄–nucleus case.
  • Annihilation mechanism: whether the two antinucleons in the d̄ annihilate independently or simultaneously, yielding the first determination of the d̄ radius and a probe of short-range pairs.
  • Cold quark matter: an anomalously large annihilation phase space would signal direct participation of all valence quark–antiquark pairs — a candidate signature of a cold quark-matter state — with complementary formation studies at Belle II.

Detector Development

Several of my measurements were enabled by original detector and data-acquisition techniques that transfer directly to next-generation spectrometers:

  • High-resolution calorimetry with single-γ tagging of fast π⁰ (the enabling idea behind E73).
  • TES cryogenic microcalorimeters for sub-eV X-ray and γ spectroscopy.
  • The first energy-resolved muon-spin-spectroscopy (μSR) spectrometer — event-by-event positron-energy selection with lead-glass Cherenkov calorimeters, demonstrated at J-PARC MLF with a 26% figure-of-merit gain at no cost in beam time (Nucl. Instrum. Meth. A, in press).
  • A waveform-analysis method for drift chambers reaching ~40 μm position resolution in simulation.
  • Streaming data acquisition for near-real-time analysis, and a plate-Cherenkov detector for particle identification.
